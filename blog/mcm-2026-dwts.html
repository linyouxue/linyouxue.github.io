00 · Project overview

The visible scores were only half the system

Dancing with the Stars combines expert scores with public votes, but the public vote totals are never released. That hidden variable sits at the center of every controversy: a technically weak celebrity can survive through popularity, while a strong performer can leave after a single unstable week.

Our solution treats fan support as a latent time series constrained by observed eliminations. We first estimate weekly vote shares and their uncertainty. We then hold each historical schedule fixed and replay the season under two aggregation rules. A Micro-Macro Random Forest framework explains weekly scoring and final survival, and the final PGHM design converts the evidence into an operational voting system.

Task 1

Infer each contestant's weekly fan vote share from judge scores and elimination outcomes, with explicit consistency and certainty measures.

Task 2

Compare Rank and Percentage aggregation through cross-season counterfactual replay, then evaluate Bottom-2 plus Judges' Save.

Task 3

Separate short-term score drivers from long-term survival factors, including age, industry, geography, and professional partner.

Task 4

Design a weekly system that balances professional quality, audience influence, entertainment value, and operational simplicity.

Deliverable

Translate the statistical conclusions into a producer-facing recommendation that can be implemented without changing the two existing information sources: judge scores and fan votes.

01 · Preparation

Data representation and modeling assumptions

The raw file stores contestant information and week-by-week judge scores in a wide table. Missing scores are mostly structural: a contestant has already been eliminated, or a fourth judge did not exist in that season. We therefore preserve these values as missing rather than turning them into zeros.

Wide inputContestant and weekly judge columns
Long tableOne contestant-week record per row
State flagsis_active and eliminated_this_week
Schedule controlWeekly elimination count ms,w

The weekly elimination count is carried into every counterfactual simulation. This holds the competition format constant, so the analysis changes only the aggregation rule rather than the pace of the season.

Temporal inertia

Popularity evolves smoothly between adjacent weeks. This supports state-space smoothing while still allowing larger shocks through process noise.

Judge-score error

Judge scores mainly reflect technical ability, with subjective deviations treated as approximately zero-mean noise.

Elimination as a hard fact

Reconstructed votes must remain compatible with observed eliminations. QP calibration enforces this logical boundary.

Vote redistribution

In full-season simulations, votes from eliminated contestants are redistributed in proportion to the remaining contestants' relative popularity.

02 · Task 1

Reconstructing hidden fan votes with SSPF-QP

The vote reconstruction problem is underdetermined: many vote distributions can produce the same eliminated contestant. The solution must combine a statistical prior over how popularity moves with a logical constraint from what actually happened.

Step 1: align judge scores and fan votes on one scale

For active contestant i in season s, week w, we first aggregate all available judge scores and normalize them within that week. This removes changes in judge count and scoring scale across seasons.

Ji,s,w = Σj judge_scorei,s,w,j
qi,s,w = Ji,s,w / Σk∈A(s,w) Jk,s,w,   Σi∈A(s,w) qi,s,w = 1q is the contestant's share of all judge points awarded that week.

Step 2: model fan support as a latent temporal state

Fan shares must be nonnegative and sum to one. We introduce an unconstrained latent state h and map it to the probability simplex with Softmax. The first week uses an approximately uniform prior, and a random walk represents week-to-week popularity inertia.

pi,w = exp(hi,w) / Σk∈A(w) exp(hk,w)
hi,1 ~ N(0, 0.1²),   hi,w = hi,w-1 + ηi,w,   ηi,w ~ N(0, σ²)σ controls how rapidly popularity is allowed to change.

Step 3: turn an elimination into a smooth likelihood

Judge share and fan share are combined on the same scale. If Ew is eliminated, its composite score should be close to the lowest score among all active contestants. A sigmoid likelihood rewards particles consistent with that ordering without assigning zero probability to an upset.

Si,w = λqi,w + (1 - λ)pi,w
mw = mink∈A(w), k≠E(w)(Sk,w - SE(w),w),   Lw = sigmoid(κmw)κ controls how strongly the observed elimination reshapes particle weights.

Step 4: sequential filtering, prediction, and strict calibration

1PropagateAdvance every particle using the random-walk state transition.
2PredictUse the prior mean to predict the elimination before seeing the current label.
3UpdateWeight and resample particles using the observed elimination likelihood.
4CalibrateMove the posterior mean as little as possible while enforcing strict consistency.
minp̂(w) ½‖pw - p̂w‖²subject to Σp̂i,w=1, p̂i,w≥0, and SE(w),w(p̂) ≤ Sj,w(p̂)-δ for every j≠E(w).

This is a convex quadratic program. It preserves the particle filter's statistical estimate as closely as possible while guaranteeing that the eliminated contestant is lowest under the inference rule. Posterior variance remains available as an uncertainty measure:

Certaintyi,w = 1 - Var(pi,w) / [Σk∈A(w)Var(pk,w) + ε]

Parameter selection and validation

A small grid search optimized one-step-ahead elimination accuracy, computed before incorporating the current week's elimination. The chosen setting balances judge and fan influence and avoids temporal leakage.

Interpretation: close or surprising eliminations leave many plausible vote distributions, so posterior variance rises. Stable high- or low-support trajectories concentrate the particles and produce greater certainty. The lower external consistency under Rank is also expected: the latent model works in continuous share space, while Rank discards magnitude and keeps only order.
03 · Task 2

Counterfactual replay: Rank versus Percentage

Task 2 does not train another prediction model. It uses the same calibrated fan shares from Task 1 and changes only the rule that combines judges and fans. For every historical week, the active field and real number of eliminations are fixed.

Percentage method

Preserves the size of score differences by adding judge and fan shares directly. The lowest composite score is eliminated.

Spcti,s,w = λqi,s,w + (1-λ)p̂i,s,w

Rank method

Converts each continuous share to an ordinal position, then adds judge rank and fan rank. Average rank resolves ties, followed by judge rank.

Ri,s,w = rJi,s,w + rFi,s,w

The core structural distinction is information loss. Percentage retains the gap between first and second place; Rank treats a narrow gap and an overwhelming gap as the same one-position difference.

Changing the aggregation rule alters who leaves in roughly one of every three elimination weeks. Repeating the calculation for λ∈[0.4,0.6] leaves the magnitude stable, so the effect comes from ordinal compression versus linear superposition, not from a finely tuned weight.

Does either rule consistently favor the audience?

We measure the fan-rank position of eliminated contestants under each rule. The point estimate suggests Rank is somewhat more favorable to audience preferences, but propagating Task 1's posterior uncertainty makes that direction unstable.

Δ = median(rFelim,pct) - median(rFelim,rank)
Uncertainty result: Δ has a 90% interval of [-1, 0]. Across perturbations, P(Δ<0)=0.1125 and P(Δ=0)=0.8875. We therefore avoid using Δ alone for the recommendation and rely more heavily on the stable divergence rate D, case analysis, and institutional risk.

Judges' Professional Bottom-line Assumption

The historical data does not contain judges' actual Bottom-2 decisions, so we introduce an interpretable proxy, JPBA. The synthesis rule first selects the Bottom-2; judges then eliminate the contestant with the lower weekly judge total. Professional alignment is measured by whether the eliminated contestant belongs to the bottom 20% of judge rankings.

Recommendation from Task 2: use Percentage as the trigger because it more readily surfaces fan-strong but judge-weak outliers, then restrict judges' corrective power to the Bottom-2. If no Save is adopted, Rank is the safer standalone rule. The trade-off is explicit: professional alignment rises, but fan influence at the critical elimination boundary falls.
04 · Task 3

Micro-Macro Random Forest analysis

Weekly excellence and season-long survival are different outcomes. A contestant may receive good scores in one week yet lack persistent audience support; another may survive many rounds despite weak technique. We therefore separate the analysis into a weekly micro layer and a final-outcome macro layer.

State-feature separation

Dynamic state

Previous-week judge score Mt-1, cumulative consistency Ct, and normalized schedule progress T.

Static traits

Age, industry category, and professional dance partner identity.

Geography

Non-US contestants form an international group; US states are mapped to West, South, Midwest, and Northeast.

This design controls for current technical form before attributing an effect to age, occupation, or partner. It also avoids sparse state-level categories while retaining broad regional voting patterns.

Micro layer: judges and fans use different standards

qi,s,w = fRF(Mi,s,w-1, Ci,s,w, Ts,w, Agei, Industryi, Partneri, Geographyi)
p̂i,s,w = gRF(Mi,s,w-1, Ci,s,w, Ts,w, Agei, Industryi, Partneri, Geographyi)
1

Weekly outcomes are overwhelmingly dynamic

Dynamic momentum and consistency account for 88.7% of judge-side importance and 92.8% of fan-side importance. Static identity variables explain much less of a single week's result.

2

Judges exhibit strong technical inertia

Consistency Ct carries more than 70% importance in the judge model. Fan support is far less sensitive to this technical hierarchy and more exposed to emotional or narrative effects.

3

Age bias is asymmetric

Age correlates with judge outcomes at approximately r=-0.30, compared with about r=-0.15 for fan outcomes. Fans are roughly 50% more tolerant of older contestants.

4

The professional-partner dividend is judge-facing

Top partners can use choreography to conceal celebrity weaknesses and lift judge evaluations, but this advantage loses marginal power when translated into broad public popularity.

5

Geography is weak within a single week

Geographic features contribute less than 2% at the micro level. A hometown does not directly produce a high weekly score, even though geography matters later for cumulative survival.

Macro layer: predict season survival, not a weekly score

The Normalized Survival Index maps the first elimination to 0 and the champion to 1, making final placements comparable across seasons with different field sizes.

Age is the largest long-term obstacle, with age and NSI correlated at approximately r=-0.403. Industry ranks next: athletes benefit from discipline and technical transfer, while reality stars benefit from existing fan bases. Geography rises from negligible weekly importance to 14.6% of final-survival importance, consistent with a stable regional voting-bloc effect.

Micro-Macro conclusion: judges are more technical, age-sensitive, and responsive to choreography; fans are more tolerant of age and more responsive to identity and regional affiliation. Fan voting acts as a counterweight to the judges' technical hierarchy, but long-term survival still accumulates structural advantages.
05 · Task 4

PGHM: a Professional Guardrail Hybrid Mechanism

The final system changes neither information source. It keeps judge scores and fan votes, but reorganizes how they determine risk, who receives corrective review, and when the audience has complete control.

1 · Percentage scoring

Preserve score magnitudes so a very weak technical performance cannot be hidden by ordinal compression.

+
2 · Bottom-2 Dance-Off

The two lowest composite scores perform again; judges eliminate the technically weaker pair.

+
3 · Fan-only finale

After the technical guardrail has shaped the finalist field, the audience alone chooses the champion.

If a week eliminates more than one contestant, the Bottom-2 and Dance-Off sequence repeats. After one contestant leaves, scores are recalculated over the remaining field before the next elimination.

06 · Model analysis

Robustness, strengths, and limitations

We propagated Task 1 uncertainty through 1,000 Monte Carlo simulations. Under perturbations drawn from posterior variance, the result-reversal rate between rules remained stable at a mean of 15.4% with a standard deviation of 1.2%. The rule comparison therefore does not arise from one fragile vote reconstruction.

Boundary stress tests also targeted the cases the proposed rule is explicitly intended to fix. PGHM removed the technical outlier Bobby Bones in Week 4 and retained the high-scoring Sabrina Bryan in Week 6.

Strengths

  • A reusable counterfactual engine replays complete historical seasons under alternative rules.
  • The Micro-Macro framework separates weekly technical mechanisms from final survival.
  • Particle posteriors quantify uncertainty, while QP makes logical consistency explicit and reproducible.

Limitations

  • The inference layer is continuous and therefore fits Percentage more naturally than discrete ordinal Rank.
  • A random-walk popularity prior cannot fully reproduce rare viral shocks, scandals, or abrupt media events.
  • JPBA is an interpretable proxy because actual judges' Bottom-2 decisions are unavailable in the supplied data.
07 · Conclusion

From hidden votes to an operational rule

The project builds one continuous evidence chain. SSPF-QP reconstructs weekly fan shares without pretending they are directly observed. Counterfactual replay shows that aggregation is not a cosmetic detail: it changes eliminations in about one-third of weeks. Random forests reveal why judges and fans systematically disagree, and PGHM converts those differences into a bounded, explainable intervention.

A credible competition format should preserve audience voice, expose technical outliers, and make every corrective power narrow enough to explain.
