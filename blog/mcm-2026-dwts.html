01 · The challenge

Inferring what the show never reveals

Judge scores are public, but weekly audience votes are not. Only the contestants who advance or leave are observed. That makes the central variable of the competition invisible—and turns any comparison of elimination rules into an inverse problem.

We used 34 seasons of historical data to reconstruct weekly vote shares, replay past eliminations under alternative rules, study contestant-level effects, and propose a practical hybrid mechanism.

How can an unobserved vote distribution be recovered from visible scores and elimination outcomes—without contradicting what actually happened?
02 · Modeling system

Four connected layers

The project combines constrained statistical inference with counterfactual simulation and interpretable machine learning. Each layer answers one part of the problem while passing a consistent representation of audience support to the next.

01

SSPF-QP Reconstruction

A state-space particle filter captures week-to-week support inertia; quadratic programming minimally calibrates estimates to satisfy observed eliminations.

02

Counterfactual Replay

Historical schedules are held fixed while ranking-based and percentage-based aggregation rules are swapped and replayed across seasons.

03

Macro–Micro Analysis

Weekly and season-level random-forest models separate short-term scoring effects from longer-term survival patterns.

04

PGHM Design

A percentage-based aggregation rule is paired with a judges' save restricted to the bottom two contestants.

03 · Results

What the reconstruction revealed

01

Reliable reconstruction—with visible uncertainty

The reconstructed votes reached 0.883 external consistency in percentage-based seasons and 0.639 in ranking-based seasons. Posterior variance records where the hidden votes remain uncertain.

02

The rule matters in roughly one out of three weeks

Counterfactual replay produced the same eliminated contestant in 67.05% of elimination weeks; the remaining 32.95% changed under the alternative rule.

03

Each rule creates a different kind of influence

Percentage aggregation preserves intensity and can amplify extreme popularity. Rank aggregation compresses score gaps, making small ordering changes more consequential.

04

Performance and persistence are not the same outcome

The two-level random-forest analysis distinguishes factors affecting weekly scores and support from those associated with final season survival, including age, occupation, professional partner, and dynamic performance signals.

04 · System design

A hybrid rule with a clear boundary

Our proposed PGHM keeps audience preference meaningful through percentage-based aggregation, then gives judges a narrowly defined intervention: save one contestant only from the bottom two. The boundary limits extreme outcomes without allowing judges to override the public vote across the entire field.

05 · Reflection

What I took away

The hardest part was not choosing a complex model. It was keeping every layer logically consistent: estimated votes had to respect observed eliminations, comparisons had to preserve the original schedule, and the final recommendation needed to be understandable enough to operate on a live show.

Good modeling does more than fit hidden data—it makes uncertainty explicit and turns competing values into a rule people can actually use.
